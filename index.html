The critique examines Qi Li's 2020 chapter on data visualization in "Embodying Data." It encompasses definitions, concepts, and sub-fields like information and scientific visualization. The chapter traces data visualization's evolution from written to digital formats, highlighting its impressive growth. The interpretations of data visualization's definition by scholars Card, Mackinlay, Schneiderman, and others converge on the use of technology to present raw data in an understandable format. This emphasizes the importance of visual representation in conveying complex information. Modern data visualization has transcended representation to encompass exploration, visualization, and analysis. The use of tools like D3, a JavaScript library, exemplifies this transformation. By employing D3's dynamic race bar chart as an example, the critique underscores the interactivity, scalability, and user engagement that contemporary visualization offers. The chart's real-time interaction, animation control, and adaptability to different datasets demonstrate the potential of modern data visualization tools to make data more accessible and meaningful. Data visualization's historical context is explored, highlighting its role in education through maps, charts, and other forms. The chapter reveals that data visualization has emerged as a crucial research component in diverse fields, from algorithms to computer vision. It emphasizes the utility of visualization as a means to comprehend complex concepts and patterns. Information and scientific visualization are differentiated in terms of purpose and design. Information visualization, seen in graphs and timetables, focuses on effective communication by presenting objective data. In contrast, scientific visualization involves investigating data through simulations, waveforms, and volumes, often employing 3D modeling due to the multidimensional nature of scientific data.
